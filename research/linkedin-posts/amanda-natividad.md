## Post 1
Link: https://www.linkedin.com/in/amandanat/

Someone asked me why I post my marketing insights and POV so freely. Why I 
don't do more to capture leads. Honestly... I didn't have a great answer 
for it.

I finally just said, "Because... I think more people should just care 
about doing marketing thoughtfully, and I care more about making that 
impact than getting the form fill."

Do I want more followers? Sure. More newsletter subscribers, more podcast 
listeners, sure sure. More SparkToro customers? Yes, please. 😂 And more 
book buyers? YES.

But really, I just believe in this:
- Getting and sustaining attention is super hard, and it's only getting 
harder.
- Big Tech scrapes all your content anyway, AI is only accelerating this, 
and it sucks for us but in the meantime we would be wise to optimize for 
impressions and ongoing engagement.
- It's becoming more and more important to know where your audience is 
actually hanging out online, what they care about, what they search for, 
and what topics they're interested in... so you can do marketing that 
actually resonates with them. In a way that is sustainable for your 
business and for your sanity.

Ultimately, I care more about getting people to believe and practice these 
things than I do about squeezing a conversion out of every idea I share, 
and tracing it back to perfect attribution.

So yes, please buy my book and buy a SparkToro account. But also... it's 
ok if you don't. Just do your best to do thoughtful marketing, make smart 
bets, be good to your customers, unclench your jaw, and go drink some 
water.

**Notes:** She basically explains why she gives away free advice instead of gating everything, and it doesn't come off as fake modesty — feels like an actual belief, not a growth hack.

## Post 2
Link: https://www.linkedin.com/in/amandanat/

"How would you start a marketing strategy from scratch?"

(Real question I got in a webinar this morning, and I didn't have a chance 
to answer it, so I'm answering it here.)

The annoying but true answer is: it depends. Obviously. But because that's 
less fun, I would start with one question:

1) How is this business supposed to grow?

Is the intended growth mechanism raw discovery? Meaning: people have a 
problem, they search for it, and we need to show up. Cool. Then SEO, 
comparison pages, and educational content probably matter a lot.

Is it product-led growth? Meaning: users become a force multiplier, and 
they ultimately need to share the product with others or use the product 
more frequently in order to gain more value? Then onboarding content, 
use-case content, templates, lifecycle emails, and activation loops 
probably matter more.

Is it sales-led? Meaning: the business depends on reps reaching out, 
building relationships, educating prospects, and moving accounts through a 
long buying process. Then the first content priority might be the sales 
asset the team has been begging for, like a buyer guide or follow-up email 
sequence.

2) After this, I would look for the shortest path to proving marketing can 
help. It might look like:
- Discovery-led motion → fix the highest-intent pages
- Sales-led motion → create the asset sales needs this month
- Product-led motion → improve the content that shortens time-to-value

Of course, the long-term work matters. You still need the bigger strategy 
— the category POV, event program, content engine, etc. But early on, 
marketing needs to earn internal trust. The fastest way to do that is to 
understand how the company grows, then make that motion work better.

**Notes:** This reads like she's answering a real question from a real person, not writing a generic "here's my framework" post. Makes it feel more useful.

## Post 3
Link: https://www.linkedin.com/in/amandanat/

I don't believe in launch days.

Sure, a launch technically happens on one day — someone flips a switch and 
the thing goes live. But the best launches play out over weeks, sometimes 
months.

A launch isn't an announcement. It's a sequence:

1/ You start with the people most likely to care: the customers who asked 
for this exact thing, the ones who've been waiting, the people already 
invested enough to get excited. That first wave is where your social proof 
comes from — the testimonials, the early use cases, the screenshots of 
people using it and liking it.

2/ From there you widen the circle to your broader customer base: people 
who already trust you and use your product, many of them a nudge away 
from upgrading, going deeper, or finally trying what they've been 
circling for months.

3/ Then come prospects and free users — the folks in your orbit who still 
need some context or convincing before they'll bite.

4/ And only then do you go fully public. By that point you're not getting 
up to say "we did a thing." You're saying: here's who it's for, here's how 
people are already using it, here's the proof it works.

Sometimes, you'll even get lucky when those early users hype you up. 
They'll see the public posts and jump into the comments on their own — 
"we've been using this for a week and love it," "this fixed X for our 
team," "finally!" You can't manufacture that, but you can try to nudge it 
to happen.

It happens when you treat a launch as a rollout instead of a date on the 
calendar.

**Notes:** The "launch isn't a day, it's a sequence" idea is simple but I hadn't thought about it that way before. Good one to keep in mind for the playbook later.
