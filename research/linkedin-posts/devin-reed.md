I just crossed 100,000 followers!! 🤯

I started with like 2,000 connections and no clue what I was doing. Just 
posting about how I was bad at sales.

Some posts completely flopped. Like single-digit likes that convinced me 
the algorithm was broken. That part sucked. But then one would pop off, 
and I'd be refreshing my notifications like a psycho for three days 
straight.

The weird part? The number doesn't feel that different. 50k, 75k, 100k — 
it's all just a number on a screen. Part of me feels like it's just a 
vanity metric. But to me, it shows my commitment. It ain't easy showing up 
consistently for 6 years.

I've said smart things. I've said stupid things. I've been called out, 
called names, and cat-called (sike) I've changed jobs, survived algo 
changes, trolls, and bosses who hated that I post here.

LinkedIn really has changed my career and life in ways I never thought 
possible. I was able to build a business, become a speaker, and add 
stamps to my passport. That's the part I didn't expect.

But what keeps me going is getting DMs telling me a post helped them think 
differently about content. Marketers reaching out to work together. 
Conversations that turned into real friendships and opportunities.

So yeah — thank you for rockin' with me. Whether you've been following 
since the early days or just showed up last week.

And if you're still grinding it out here wondering if it's worth it... it 
is. But not for the reasons you think.

## Post 2 
Link: https://www.linkedin.com/in/devinreed/

I've never done website visitor ID before. And I'm low-key nervous about 
it. De-anonymizing someone and then cold emailing them feels... risky. 
Like I could easily cross the line into creepy territory. (Then again, I 
used to send ice cold emails to people who didn't know me *at all*.)

But I also don't want to waste the traffic I'm generating from content. So 
here's the workflow I'm setting up with ZoomInfo:

Instead of doing sales outreach to everyone who hits my site (the obvious 
trap), I'm trying a "Godfather play" — make an offer they can't refuse.

The idea:
↳ Visitor hits my site
↳ ZoomInfo identifies + enriches them
↳ Based on their persona, I send a relevant content asset

Because ZoomInfo gives me the context to know 𝘸𝘩𝘰 they are, not just 𝘵𝘩𝘢𝘵 
they visited.

Example: If a Director of Content Marketing lands on my page → they get my 
content strategy deck template and guide.

I shan't pitch. No "let's hop on a call." Just good ol' fashioned value. My 
goal is to start a conversation or get them into my newsletter. Def not 
assume they're ready to buy anything.

I'm sharing this because I genuinely don't know if it'll work. And I'd 
rather build this in public than pretend I have all the answers. I'll 
report back in 30 days with what happens.

Have you done something similar? What's worked? What would you do 
differently?

## Post 3 
Link: https://www.linkedin.com/in/devinreed/

You shouldn't write like a journalist, analyst, or "subject matter 
expert." The best marketers write like Hollywood screenwriters.

Because the goal isn't to inform — it's to make your audience *feel* 
something first, then land the lesson.

Here's the difference in practice:

⛔️ Most B2B content: "Here's what the data says about discovery calls..."

✅ Great content: "You're 5 minutes into a discovery call. Your buyer just 
said your price is too high. Your palms are sweating. What do you do?"

Same topic. Totally different reaction. One gets skimmed. The other gets 
saved.

At Gong, this was our secret weapon. We didn't lead with statistics, 
benchmarks, or product features. We led with moments our audience lived 
every single day — and then delivered the lesson once we had their full 
attention.

Emotion first. Data second. Every time.

The result? Buyers showed up to demos already trusting us. Sales reps 
shared our content on their own calls. Pipeline grew without us ever 
making a single ask.

Your buyers don't want to be educated. They want to feel understood. Write 
content that makes them say "that's exactly what I'm going through!" — and 
they'll never stop reading.
