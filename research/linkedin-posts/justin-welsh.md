## Post 1 
Link: https://www.linkedin.com/in/justinwelsh

I'm basically unemployable. But for years, I chased corporate safety. Keep it 
clean. Keep it impressive. Keep myself hireable. That kind of thinking kept 
me from ever chasing my entrepreneurial spirit.

Then, one day, I finally reached a breaking point. And once I finally took 
the plunge, that thinking flipped. I realized that the skills you build as 
an entrepreneur ARE your safety net.

I'd actually argue that the more you build and succeed, the better you look 
on paper. Companies kill for creative thinkers. Self-taught marketers. 
Do'ers. People who can start something from nothing and make it work. 
That's what building your own thing turns you into.

So I'm not unemployable because no one would hire me. I'm unemployable 
because I'd never go back. Once you can create your own income, a job is 
the riskier bet, not the safe one.

The good news is that entrepreneurship isn't some personality trait you're 
born with. It comes down to a specific set of skills almost nobody is 
taught, because the whole system is built to keep you employable instead.

I spent this past year writing them all down. I analyzed 250+ essays, hours 
of voice notes, and 10 interviews with people who made themselves 
unemployable on purpose. I turned it into a 30-page field manual on building 
my $15M business without wrecking my life to do it.

Link: https://www.linkedin.com/in/justinwelsh

Action beats intelligence. So be the "dumb" action person. The person who 
starts the messy and ships the imperfect thing. You'll learn more in a 
month than the person who studies it for a year.

The fact is, reality is a faster teacher than theory ever will be. 
Especially in business. The one who starts that awkward little company 
figures out what actually works because the market corrects them in real 
time. The one who studies and pontificates and collects theories...just 
stays stuck.

This happens to overly intelligent people constantly. They want to 
understand everything before they begin. So they research, plan, and 
prepare. They just never start.

Meanwhile, the "dumb" guy who just started is three years into learning 
things no book could teach you. The lessons that actually build a business 
don't come from theory. They come from doing it, getting it wrong, and 
adjusting.

Over the past year, I took everything I've learned from eight years of

short, 30-page guide you can read in an afternoon.

So you can get a little info...and get started. A summary of 250+ essays, 
dozens of voice memos, and 10 interviews with people who actually did it. 
No frameworks. No academic fluff. Just the 7 skills you need to build the 
real thing.

## Post 3
Link: https://www.linkedin.com/in/justinwelsh

Every good thing I've built started with a slightly crazy belief in myself. 
That some no-name, state school salesperson could run revenue at a company 
doing $50M+. That one person with a laptop could build a real business with 
no team and no investors. That people would pay to read what I think.

None of those thoughts were reasonable at one time. I had no evidence for 
any of them. That's the cool thing about confidence. You can't let it 
arrive after the proof. You need it before...or you never start.

You have to believe before it's earned, then act on it, and let the results 
catch up to the belief. Most people want a guarantee before they'll move. 
So they don't move. The ones who build anything bet on a version of 
themselves that doesn't quite exist yet. Then they grow into it.

That unreasonable belief is how my one-person business crossed $15M. This 
past year, I documented what separates the useful kind of delusion from the 
kind that just gets you hurt. 250+ of my own essays, dozens of voice memos, 
10 interviews with people who bet on themselves and won.

A 30-page field manual on building a real business without burning your 
life down to do it. No fake confidence. No manifesting nonsense. Just the 7 
capacities that turned an unreasonable bet into a real thing.
