It's been 9 months since I left VC to become a solopreneur. Here's an 
honest look at how it's going:

There are certainly some high's — I'm posting this from the coast of 
Croatia (say 👋 if you're at SaaStanak). The flexibility is wonderful. The 
work feels more meaningful than I've ever experienced.

But here's what I usually don't share on LinkedIn.

1️⃣ I've gone through 3 business entities (sole proprietor > LLC > S-corp) 
with increasing amounts of paperwork to deal with. S-corp has been 
especially fun (said nobody), but I'm hoping it sticks.

2️⃣ I missed what was supposed to be my first official payroll 🤦‍♂️.

3️⃣ I'm perpetually confused by sales tax on newsletter subscriptions. Some 
countries mandate registration after a single transaction. The complexity 
& cost of staying compliant globally is way more than I expected.

4️⃣ I got rejected by multiple insurance companies before I managed to get 
covered. People just don't know how to underwrite to a media + consulting 
business (in 2026…).

5️⃣ It took about 65 emails and 94 days to get paid for a $10k invoice. Now 
that's something I'd love for AI to automate.

—

There are way more "bangs head against wall" days than selfies from 
Croatia, and I wish more people talked about those. But don't get me 
wrong, I'm not looking back.

## Post 2 
Link: https://www.linkedin.com/in/kyle-poyar/

NEW: Data from 230 companies shows what's actually happening in AI 
monetization. Hope you're ready for AI credits 👀

I'm so excited to finally release the new Growth Unhinged State of B2B 
Monetization report. See it for yourself here: https://lnkd.in/eBqNmk-6

Six takeaways:

1. Hybrid pricing continues to be the most popular pricing model with 37% 
adoption. The rise of AI, and AI agents in particular, means value gets 
decoupled from customers needing to buy more seats. Companies are 
responding with hybrid pricing, which combines two or more pricing models 
like a per-seat subscription with AI consumption on top.

2. Nobody is happy with their pricing. The biggest complaint: not enough 
expansion revenue. This was the most frequently cited pricing challenge 
overall, and was a particularly acute challenge among anyone with flat-fee 
subscriptions or seat-based pricing.

3. 2026 is poised to have even more pricing shakeups. 3-in-4 software 
companies changed pricing within the last year. What's unusual: the 
largest companies are now making the most pricing changes. It's nearly 
impossible to find a >$50M ARR company that's not rethinking both pricing 
and packaging for AI. Salesforce, Anthropic, HubSpot, OpenAI, Clay, Figma, 
Canva, SAP, and GitHub all made big pricing changes just in the first few 
months of 2026.

4. AI credit adoption today is at 29%. A whopping 33% of companies say 
they plan to introduce AI credits within 6-12 months. Among companies with 
>$50M ARR, 1-in-2 (!) said they plan to introduce AI credits this year. My 
two cents: AI credits are great for vendors. They can become a nightmare 
for customers. For now I see credits as a lifeline -- just not the AI 
pricing endgame.

5. AI margins are only 50%. Internal costs were the single most important 
factor when pricing AI capabilities. Very few are seeing SaaS-like margins 
of 70-80%+.

6. AI spend is mostly cannibalizing SaaS budgets. 70% said AI comes out of 
customers' technology budget. If AI is going to deliver its full potential 
-- and justify some truly astounding valuations -- there will need to be a 
significant increase in technology spending. This probably needs to 
involve tapping into net-new pockets like headcount or services spending.

## Post 3
Link: https://www.linkedin.com/in/kyle-poyar/

Content marketing was supposed to be one of the first jobs wiped out by 
AI. Against all odds, it's thriving.

The Wall Street Journal says 50,000 companies (!) are hiring for 
"storytelling" skills. Tech companies used to acquire products; now 
they're acquiring writers & media brands (see: OpenAI & TBPN, Plaid & This 
Week in Fintech, HubSpot & Futurepedia).

But this isn't 2010s era content marketing. It's creator-led storytelling.

I partnered with beehiiv to study how the best creators & brands crack the 
code. We've observed 5 archetypes of great storytelling in 2026. Read the 
full deep dive in Growth Unhinged here: https://lnkd.in/ezxiq2bS

1. The Advocate
👉 What it is: Leading voice for your product ecosystem
👉 Examples: Creator Spotlight, Mostly Media (CJ Gustafson)
👉 Best-fit content: Third-person narratives

2. The Analyst
👉 What it is: Use data to become the trusted source in your space
👉 Examples: Growth Unhinged (😉), Peter Walker at Carta, Adam Schoenfeld 
at Keyplay, Ara Kharazian at Ramp, ChartMogul
👉 Best-fit content: Data studies

3. The Teacher*
👉 What it is: Create frameworks & how-to guides that teach people
👉 Examples: Anthony Pierri at FletchPMM, Milly Tamati (Generalist World), 
Cecilia Ziniti at GC AI
👉 Best-fit content: Invented concepts
*I think this is the most underutilized archetype in B2B right now

4. The Provocateur
👉 What it is: Advocate for something that goes against conventional 
beliefs
👉 Examples: Adam Robinson at RB2B, Codie A. Sanchez at Contrarian 
Thinking
👉 Best-fit content: Contrarian content*
*This shouldn't be another hot (lukewarm) take! Better would be a bold 
claim that's supported by experience & that others can rally around

5. The Builder
👉 What it is: Build in public, showing how you run your business
👉 Examples: Tyler Denk at beehiiv, Clay, Lovable
👉 Best-fit content: First-person narratives

Being great at storytelling matters whether you're launching your first 
product or on the verge of IPO. I hope this helps you level up.
