# Amanda Natividad — YouTube Transcript

## Video: "The Marketing Stack Era Is Over — And Most People Haven't Noticed"
Link: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gLwhOCOf86A
Channel: Amanda Natividad (ZeroClick Marketing)

---

The best-of-breed marketing stack era is ending, and most people haven't 
noticed yet.

### How we got from monoliths to unbundled SaaS stacks
In the era of Marketo, Eloqua, and old-school HubSpot, marketers used 
monolithic software — one platform that did email, landing pages, lead 
scoring, and reporting all in one place. It was expensive and clunky, and 
mediocre at most of those things, but it was one thing.

Around 2015–2016, that shifted. SaaS got cheap, APIs opened up, and Zapier 
made it trivially easy to wire tools together. The smart move became: don't 
buy one platform, pick the best tool for each job, and stitch them 
together. That's the unbundled stack — Klaviyo for email, Postscript for 
SMS, Clearbit for enrichment, HubSpot for CRM, Calendly for scheduling. 
Each tool was better at its one job than the equivalent module inside an 
old monolithic platform. For roughly a decade (2015–2024), that math 
worked — it was the right answer for the conditions of that time.

### What changed in a zero-click world
The conditions changed. Traffic is shrinking — Google AI Overviews now 
answer roughly two-thirds of searches on-page. Small publishers lost about 
60% of their Google search traffic over the past two years. Social 
platforms actively punish linking out. ChatGPT summarizes blog posts before 
anyone opens them. The old foundation — create content, get traffic, 
capture leads, convert leads — cracked. Cost per lead went way up, 
conversion windows got tighter, and top-of-funnel lead volume is a 
fraction of what it was three years ago.

The unbundled stack (Klaviyo + Postscript + Clearbit + HubSpot + Zapier + 
Calendly + Stripe) was designed for cheap, plentiful traffic. When a 
thousand leads a week came in, it was fine to lose 10% to tool handoff 
issues — an SMS platform not syncing with the email tool's unsubscribes, a 
CRM sync breaking overnight unnoticed for days. 900 good leads still made 
it through. Now, with only a few hundred leads trickling in weekly, every 
single one has to land. Losing 10% to a stack held together with 
"duct tape and a prayer" is no longer affordable.

In an abundance economy, the cost of stitching tools together was low and 
the gain from best-in-class features was high. In a scarcity economy, 
stitching costs more, because every lead that falls through the cracks is 
more expensive to replace.

### The Clearbit example
Amanda used to love Clearbit — capture just a lead's email, and it would 
pull back job title, company, headcount, industry, even LinkedIn. One 
question on a form, 15 answers back automatically. In 2023, HubSpot bought 
Clearbit and eventually folded it into HubSpot as a feature — great if you 
use HubSpot, a loss if you don't. For two years, nobody built a standalone 
replacement accessible to small teams. Then she discovered a version of 
Clearbit-style enrichment had been built directly inside Typeform's new 
"Growth Flow" feature — enriching form submissions in the background via 
third-party data providers, with reported match rates up to 92% for B2B 
and 71% for B2C.

### The hidden cost of handoffs (DTC skincare example)
A typical DTC brand's quiz-funnel backend usually looks like this: quiz 
lives in the form tool → automation pushes the lead into the ESP → ESP 
sends the welcome email → another automation pushes the lead into the CRM 
→ a third automation pushes high-intent leads into the SMS tool → a fourth 
automation pings Slack for high-LTV leads → a fifth tool handles 
enrichment into a sixth dashboard and seventh database.

That's five handoffs — five places a lead can die, five subscriptions, 
five logins, and a whole funnel that can silently break at 2am if one 
tool's API changes.

With a consolidated tool like Typeform's Growth Flow, all of that lives 
inside one platform: the quiz fires, the contact record creates itself, 
enrichment runs in the background, the email sequence kicks off, SMS goes 
to qualified segments, the Slack ping happens, checkout happens via 
Stripe, and reporting lives in one dashboard. One tool, one bill, one place 
where the data lives.

### The caveat
Amanda is careful to note she isn't claiming Typeform beats Klaviyo at 
email or Postscript at SMS — it probably doesn't. Klaviyo has 15 years of 
email infrastructure investment; Postscript is the deepest SMS tool 
available. A $100M brand running predictive LTV models off purchase data 
should keep its dedicated stack. But most listeners aren't that brand. For 
a small team on small lead volume, running seven tools each at 60% 
utilization costs more than running one tool that does each job at 80%. 
That's the math that flipped.

### The actionable takeaway
Pull up your credit card statement. Highlight every SaaS line item that's 
part of your marketing stack. Add them up. Then ask: "What's the fewest 
tools I could run my entire customer journey through?" — not "What's the 
best tool for X or Y?" (that was the 2018 question). Some tools are 
load-bearing and worth keeping (Klaviyo at scale, Shopify for e-commerce, 
a CRM you actually use) — but most people likely have two or three tools 
on their stack that they adopted years ago and never re-evaluated. The 
answer is probably smaller than you think.
