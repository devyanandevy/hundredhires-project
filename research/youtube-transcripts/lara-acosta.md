### Why the old LinkedIn is dead
For a long time, LinkedIn worked exactly the way everyone said it did: post 
at a specific time, get comments in the first hour, end your post with a 
vague life lesson, get thousands of likes. The old system was a collection 
of separate scoring systems — one tracked trending topics, one measured how 
fast you got engagement after posting — and if you hit the right triggers, 
it pushed your content to more people. It worked, for everyone.

But then impressions started dropping — not just for small accounts. 
Creators with hundreds of thousands of followers, posting the exact same 
content that used to get millions of views, suddenly got a fraction of 
that. Nobody could figure out why. Most people's response was to double 
down on the old playbook: post more generic content, use more hashtags, 
copy big creators, post twice a day, use AI-generated copy. But the data 
told a different story — something had fundamentally changed, and doing 
more of the old thing wasn't going to fix it.

Lara started reaching out to other creators about what she was seeing, and 
most people pushed back — the "gurus" who'd built their businesses around 
the old playbook didn't want to admit it was gone. But LinkedIn published a 
research paper on the algorithm, then later withdrew it. She found it 
before it disappeared and studied it repeatedly.

### Myths the paper (and her own testing) debunked

**1. "Educational content is the best viral format"** — False. Education 
vs. storytelling doesn't reliably produce a performance difference. What 
matters most is whether the format matches how that specific person 
communicates naturally.

**2. "Text-only content is statistically the worst format"** — False. In 
the past 3 months she's seen more viral text-only posts than carousels, 
infographics, and photo+text combined — especially from beginners and 
domain experts who write clearly.

**3. "External links kill your post"** — False, and LinkedIn has confirmed 
this. That penalty existed ~2 years ago but changed. The myth persists 
because posts either lack a real story/insight and just function as a 
"click this link" prompt, or because the messaging and the link don't 
align, making the audience feel misled.

**4. "LinkedIn video doesn't work"** — False, when done right. Two things 
determine whether video works: the first three seconds need a visual hook 
that compresses the entire concept, and the post copy above the video 
needs to be around 500 words to give context for why someone should stop 
and watch.

### What actually works now

**1. Beginner accounts have a structural advantage.** The new system's 
performance gap versus the old one is largest for members with the fewest 
interactions — meaning smaller accounts benefit the most. The old system 
favored people who already had large audiences (more followers = more 
built-in reach). The new system matches content to interest instead, so a 
specific, well-written post from a small account can outperform a mediocre 
post from a big one.

**2. There are now two separate feed content types**, confirmed publicly by 
LinkedIn's senior director of product management (October 2024):
- **Connected content**: posts from people you already follow
- **Unconnected content**: content surfaced based on your interests and 
what your network engages with — from accounts you've never followed or 
interacted with. A post doesn't need to go viral to benefit from this; it 
just needs to be useful enough for someone to share or comment on it. This 
means your network isn't just your audience — it's your distribution 
system.

**3. Profiles are now read like an AI prompt.** LinkedIn converts your 
entire profile (headline, job history, skills, certifications) into text 
fed into an AI system. A vague headline like "Brand Designer" gets far less 
targeted distribution than a specific one like "I help D2C founders build 
brand identities that convert" — same person, different distribution, 
purely from how the profile is written.

**4. Relevance now beats recency.** Posts get reassessed and can resurface 
weeks after publishing if someone finds them newly relevant — similar to 
how YouTube's recommendation algorithm can resurface a year-old video. This 
means a genuinely good post can keep generating leads long after it was 
first published.

### The 5-step action framework

**Step 1 — Fix your profile.** The algorithm reads your whole profile as a 
brief every time you post. Your headline should state exactly what you do 
and who you do it for, not just your job title.

**Step 2 — Pick one lane.** One core topic (with adjacent subtopics), not 
three or five. Going off-topic — even occasionally — adds noise to the 
picture the algorithm is building of you, and hurts distribution across 
everything you post, not just that one post. Consistent signal is what lets 
the algorithm confidently place you.

**Step 3 — Match content format to how you naturally think ("content-founder 
fit").** There's no single winning format. Some people are naturally 
logical/framework thinkers; others are naturally storytellers. Content 
performs best when the format matches how the creator actually thinks and 
communicates — copying someone else's format usually underperforms.

**Step 4 — Manage your engagement habits deliberately.** What you like and 
comment on shapes who the algorithm shows you to. Engaging mostly with 
unrelated content (e.g., generic motivational posts) trains the algorithm 
to place you in that world instead of your target audience's world.

**Step 5 — Just post.** Don't wait for a perfect format or reverse-engineer 
what gurus are posting. Post something specific and honest about what you 
actually do — the algorithm needs real signal to start building a picture 
of who you are.

### Closing framework: the 3 P's
- **Positioning** — clarity of what you do, across your profile and content
- **Packaging** — presenting your knowledge in simple, easy-to-implement 
formats
- **Proof** — concrete evidence (screenshots, testimonials, results) that 
builds trust

### Final take
LinkedIn isn't dead for everyone — it's "dead" only for people still 
following the old playbook that the platform's own research has 
dismantled. Small accounts currently have a real structural advantage, and 
most creators haven't realized the window has opened.
